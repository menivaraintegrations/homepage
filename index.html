Your business runs on a combination of apps that don't talk to each other, spreadsheets that live on one person's computer, and processes that work fine until the person who knows them goes on vacation.

Process

How this works

Step 1

Discovery

We talk for 30 minutes. I ask questions, you tell me what's frustrating. No pitch, no proposal template. I'm just listening.

This is free.

Step 2

Quick Win

I scope a small, contained project — usually 1–2 weeks. Fixed price. Clear outcome. Something concrete enough that you'll know whether it worked.

This is where we find out if we're a good fit.

Step 3

Ongoing (If It Makes Sense)

If the quick win goes well, some clients want a standing arrangement — a few hours a month to handle new problems as they surface, maintain what we built, and keep iterating.

I only offer this after we've worked together. The relationship has to be earned.

Background

Who I am

I spent years running restaurants before I ended up in marketing, and then somehow in automation. The through-line is that I like figuring out how things work and fixing what doesn't.

The restaurant years taught me operations — how a business actually functions when there's no room for error, thin margins, and everything has to happen in the right order. The marketing years taught me how businesses talk to customers and what tools they actually use day-to-day.

The automation piece came from impatience. Every time I had to do the same thing twice, I'd look for a way to make it not happen again. That turned into a skill set and eventually into this.

I work with Make.com, n8n, Google Workspace, ClickUp, and whatever else the problem requires. I'm not the world's deepest expert in any single tool. I'm pretty good at figuring out what's broken and building something that fixes it.

If you have an operations degree and a team of 50, you probably don't need me. If you have a scrappy business that's outgrown its original systems, you might.
